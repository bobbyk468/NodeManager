> **Definition Box: Topological Reasoning Mapping (TRM)**
>
> Let **G = (V, E)** be a domain Knowledge Graph where **V** is a set of concept nodes and **E ⊆ V × R × V** is a set of typed edges with relation types **R**.
>
> Let **R = ⟨s₁, …, sₙ⟩** be a large reasoning model's chain-of-thought for a student answer, where each step **sᵢ** is a natural-language proposition.
>
> ---
>
> **Definition 1 — TRM Step Mapping.** A Topological Reasoning Map is a projection function
> $$\phi(s_i) = N_i$$
> that assigns to each step **sᵢ** a subset **Nᵢ ⊆ V** representing the KG concept nodes explicitly referenced in the step's propositional content.
>
> ---
>
> **Definition 2 — Topological Adjacency.** Two consecutive steps sᵢ, sᵢ₊₁ are *topologically adjacent* if their KG projections share at least one concept node:
> $$N_i \cap N_{i+1} \neq \emptyset$$
>
> ---
>
> **Definition 3 — Structural Leap.** A pair (sᵢ, sᵢ₊₁) constitutes a *structural leap* if both steps are grounded (Nᵢ ≠ ∅, Nᵢ₊₁ ≠ ∅) and they are not topologically adjacent:
> $$N_i \cap N_{i+1} = \emptyset$$
>
> ---
>
> **Definition 4 — Leap Count.**
> $$\text{leaps}(R, \phi) = \left|\{i : N_i \neq \emptyset,\; N_{i+1} \neq \emptyset,\; N_i \cap N_{i+1} = \emptyset\}\right|$$
>
> ---
>
> **Definition 5 — Grounding Density.**
> $$\text{density}(R, \phi) = \frac{|\{i : N_i \neq \emptyset\}|}{n} \in [0, 1]$$

**Prose immediately after the box** (separates rigorous math from the probabilistic ML):

> In addition to the topological mapping Nᵢ, our implementation overlays a semantic classification **cᵢ ∈ {SUPPORTS, CONTRADICTS, UNCERTAIN}** generated by the LRM verifier to indicate each step's propositional alignment with the domain graph (see Section 4.1). This classification is not formally derived from G — it is a prompted LRM output whose empirical reliability is evaluated in Section 5a.

**Plain-English gloss:**

- *Step Mapping* (Def 1): For each sentence in the AI's reasoning, we identify which domain concepts it mentions and record them as a set.
- *Topological Adjacency* (Def 2): Two consecutive reasoning steps are "connected" if they reference at least one concept in common.
- *Structural Leap* (Def 3): When the AI moves from one concept to a completely unrelated one — with no concept in common — it has made a structural leap, leaving an unexplained gap in the reasoning chain.
- *Leap Count* (Def 4): The total number of structural leaps in a trace. Zero means fully continuous; higher values mean more unexplained jumps.
- *Grounding Density* (Def 5): What fraction of the AI's reasoning steps are anchored to at least one domain concept? Low density means the model reasoned in general language without naming the specific concepts the rubric evaluates.

**Running example** (using Sample 0, DigiKlausur Neural Networks dataset):

> To illustrate, Figure X shows a representative trace for a student answer defining artificial neural networks. Step 10 maps to **N₁₀ = {processing\_unit}** — the LRM is reasoning about what a neural network is *made of*. Step 11 maps to **N₁₁ = {human\_brain}** — the LRM has jumped to reasoning about what a neural network *resembles*. Because N₁₀ ∩ N₁₁ = ∅, Definition 3 classifies this as a structural leap. The educator sees an amber indicator between steps 10 and 11, signaling that the LRM skipped the intermediate concepts (`learning_process`, `synaptic_weights`) that connect processing units to the brain analogy in the domain KG — precisely the pedagogical explanation a student disputing their grade would need.

## 2. §1 Introduction — Full Draft (~1,000 words)

*Target: IEEE VIS VAST double-column format, ending with 4 contribution bullets.*

Automated short-answer grading has reached human-level accuracy on structured benchmarks [Mohler 2011, Dzikovska 2016, Liu 2024], yet educators remain reluctant to rely on it in high-stakes assessments. The bottleneck is not performance — it is accountability: when a model downgrades a student's answer, the educator has no mechanism to inspect why. This opacity makes the system an oracle to be trusted or ignored, rather than a collaborator to be audited. We argue that the missing link is not a better model, but a better interface — one that projects the model's reasoning onto the educator's domain knowledge, creating a shared visual topology for co-auditing. We present **ConceptGrade**, a visual analytics system that implements this topology through **Topological Reasoning Mapping (TRM)**: a technique that projects a large reasoning model's chain-of-thought onto a domain Knowledge Graph, enabling educators to co-audit both machine reasoning and student knowledge gaps simultaneously.

The opacity problem is not incidental to AI grading — it is structural. Modern large reasoning models (LRMs) produce extensive natural-language rationale for every grading decision, yet this rationale is generated as a flat sequence of propositions disconnected from any formal domain representation. An educator reading the verdict "the student partially addresses backpropagation but misses the role of the learning rate" cannot determine whether the model correctly identified a conceptual gap, confused two related concepts, or made an inferential leap that no domain expert would sanction. Existing approaches to AI transparency in NLP and educational contexts offer partial remedies: feature attribution methods (LIME, SHAP) identify which input tokens influenced the score, while attention-based explanations visualize which parts of the reference answer the model focused on [cite]. These techniques answer "what did the model look at?" but not "was the model's reasoning *about the domain* coherent?" — a fundamentally different question that requires a structured representation of the domain itself.

This gap motivates a different design paradigm. Rather than explaining a model's behavior post-hoc, ConceptGrade situates the model's reasoning *within* the domain Knowledge Graph during grading, exposing the topological structure of its chain-of-thought in real time. When the LRM's reasoning trace jumps between disconnected KG regions — referencing `processing_unit` in one step and `human_brain` in the next, with no bridging concept — TRM flags this as a structural leap: an incomplete explanation that an educator can inspect, question, and act on. The educator's response — adding the skipped concept to the rubric — is not a correction of the model's output but an update to their own implicit mental model of what the rubric should require. This bidirectional process, in which both the AI and the human educator refine their representations of the domain, is what we term **co-auditing**.

Co-auditing is distinct from two superficially similar paradigms. *Assistive grading* is unidirectional: the AI grades, the human approves or overrides. *Interactive Machine Teaching* (IMT) [Simard 2017] involves the human updating the model's weights or decision rules. In co-auditing, neither the model's weights nor the human's final grade necessarily change — what changes is the educator's explicit understanding of the rubric, surfaced by the act of inspecting reasoning that would otherwise remain opaque. This distinction carries direct consequences for system design: the interface must not merely display the model's output but create the conditions under which an educator's implicit mental model becomes externalized and refineable. ConceptGrade realizes this through three linked panels — an LRM trace viewer, a KG subgraph, and a rubric editor — connected by bidirectional brushing: clicking a CONTRADICTS step highlights the implicated KG nodes; clicking a KG node filters the trace to steps that reference it; clicking a chip in the rubric editor logs the exact concept the educator chose to act on.

We evaluate ConceptGrade at two levels. At the accuracy level, the KG-grounded scoring pipeline reduces Mean Absolute Error by 32.4% over a pure LLM baseline on the Mohler CS benchmark (Wilcoxon p = 0.003, N = 120), and achieves a Fisher combined p = 0.003 across 1,239 student answers from three domain datasets (CS, Neural Networks, and elementary science). Non-significance on the science dataset defines the boundary condition of TRM: in domains where the KG cannot discriminate between semantically similar student answers, the grounding advantage disappears — a finding we report explicitly rather than elide. At the interaction level, a controlled user study (N = X, two conditions) demonstrates that educators exposed to TRM-rendered reasoning traces make rubric edits with a semantic alignment rate of Y% versus a hypergeometric null baseline of Z% (p < 0.05), providing quantitative evidence that the visualization facilitates mutual alignment between the machine's domain model and the educator's pedagogical mental model.

In summary, our contributions are:

1. **Topological Reasoning Mapping (TRM)** — A formal technique (Definitions 1–5, Section 3) that maps each LRM reasoning step to KG concept nodes, defines topological adjacency and structural leaps, and introduces leap count and grounding density as measurable trace properties. TRM is the first approach to operationalize reasoning-chain continuity as a visualizable topological property of an LRM's chain-of-thought.

2. **Bidirectional Co-Auditing Interface** — A visual analytics system (Section 4) that links an LRM trace panel, a KG subgraph view, and a rubric editor through DashboardContext bidirectional brushing. The Click-to-Add interaction — where educators click CONTRADICTS chips directly to flag rubric concepts — provides zero-ambiguity attribution for causal proximity analysis.

3. **Multi-Dataset ML Accuracy Evidence** — 32.4% MAE reduction on the Mohler CS benchmark (Wilcoxon p = 0.003, N = 120); Fisher combined p = 0.003 across 1,239 answers from three domain datasets. Non-significance on Kaggle ASAG (science) defines the KG discriminability boundary condition (Section 5a).

4. **Controlled User Study with Pre-Registered Causal Metrics** — A two-condition study (N = X CS/NN educators) using multi-window causal attribution (15 s / 30 s / 60 s, primary = 30 s), semantic alignment rate as the primary H2 metric, and leap count as a pre-registered exploratory moderator of H1, analyzed via GEE with exchangeable working correlation (Section 5b).

## 3. Open Questions for This Review

### Q1 — Second Paragraph: "Structural" Critique of LRM Rationale

The second paragraph argues that LRM rationale is "disconnected from any formal domain representation." A reviewer who works on Chain-of-Thought (CoT) faithfulness [Wei 2022, Lanham 2023] may push back: CoT reasoning does reference domain concepts, just implicitly. The phrase "flat sequence of propositions" is accurate but may read as dismissive of recent CoT progress.

**Question:** Should we add a concession sentence here? E.g., *"Recent work on chain-of-thought prompting [Wei 2022] demonstrates that LRMs can produce domain-accurate reasoning chains — our claim is not that the reasoning is wrong, but that its topological structure is invisible to the educator without a reference KG."*

---

### Q2 — Third Paragraph: "Implicit Mental Model" — Cite Norman?

The phrase "implicit mental model" is borrowed from Don Norman's *The Design of Everyday Things* [Norman 1988]. Using it without citation may draw a reviewer comment, but citing Norman in a VIS paper about grading may feel incongruous.

**Question:** Should we cite Norman [1988] and/or Gentner & Stevens [1983] (mental models in HCI) here, or is "implicit mental models" in this context sufficiently established jargon not to require a citation?

### Q3 — Evaluation Paragraph: Placeholder X, Y, Z

The evaluation paragraph uses X, Y, Z as placeholders for user study results. For the first draft sent to co-authors, these should either remain as placeholders with a clear note, or be filled with *hypothetical* numbers from the power analysis (e.g., "if 60% vs. 25% null baseline").

**Question:** Should the co-author draft use (a) X/Y/Z with a clearly marked TODO, (b) the hypothetical numbers from the power analysis ("based on our pilot projection, we expect Y% ≈ 60%"), or (c) omit the evaluation paragraph entirely until study data is available?

---

### Q4 — Contributions Bullet 1: "First Approach" Claim

Contribution #1 claims TRM is "the first approach to operationalize reasoning-chain continuity as a visualizable topological property." This is a strong claim that needs Related Work support. The closest prior work is:
- **Trace faithfulness** [Lanham 2023]: measures whether CoT reasoning actually caused the output, but doesn't use a domain KG
- **Knowledge-grounded NLG evaluation** [Ji 2023]: evaluates LLM outputs against KGs, but not reasoning *chains*
- **Concept map visualization** [Novak 1984, Ruiz-Primo 2004]: maps student knowledge to graphs, but not LRM reasoning

**Question:** Is the "first approach" claim defensible with this prior work, or should we soften to "to our knowledge, the first approach in the educational VA context"? A narrower scope claim is easier to defend.
